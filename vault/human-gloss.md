# The Human Gloss

> **'Meaning is the only directly experienced phenomenon' — valid exposition that points the reader at the formal claim, carrying no probative weight of its own.**
> *Part I · FT (frame-internal) · canonical*

## Definition
The human gloss is the second of the two registers into which the single axiom sentence splits. It states: *meaning is the only directly experienced phenomenon.* At the thin level, "meaning" means **structured signification affecting system behavior.** It is offered as valid exposition only — a gloss, not a separate load-bearing claim.

## In depth
The gloss is the rich, phenomenological half of the axiom — the form that addresses the human reader directly. Its job is to point that reader at the formal closure claim. It does not, however, map anything the formal claim does not; it carries no probative weight.

The rubric keeps the gloss and the formal closure claim marked as distinct registers precisely because the single sentence "does two jobs at once," and the discipline does not allow it to carry both undivided. Both registers must be preserved, and a reader must always mark which one is in use.

The defining hazard attached to the gloss is misuse in argument: drawing on the gloss's richness to make a case while retreating to the thin closure claim under challenge. That maneuver is named **axiom equivocation** and is capped by the controlling authority.

## Epistemic & alethic marks
- **E:** Derived, FT exposition — a gloss, not a separate load-bearing claim.
- **A:** Carries no probative weight; points the human reader at the formal claim without mapping anything the formal claim does not.

## Distinctions & cautions
The gloss is NOT a second load-bearing claim and must not be defended as one. It is NOT a substitute for the formal closure claim. The prohibited move is arguing from this rich gloss while defending with the thin closure claim — axiom equivocation.
