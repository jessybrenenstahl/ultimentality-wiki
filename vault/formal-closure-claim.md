# The Formal Closure Claim

> **The accessible state space of a participant is closed under symbolic transformation — falsifiable in principle.**
> *Part I · AC (authority-canonical) · canonical*

## Definition
The formal closure claim is the first of the two registers into which the single axiom sentence splits. It states: *the accessible state space of a participant is closed under symbolic transformation.* Every accessible state is structured signification; participation occurs through a finite set of directional operations over that structured signification; and there is no extra-symbolic access channel available to the participant as participant.

## In depth
The single axiom sentence "does two jobs at once, and the discipline does not allow it to carry both undivided." The rubric splits it into a **formal** register (this claim) and a **human gloss**. This formal closure claim is the thin, load-bearing half — the one a defender is entitled to lean on under challenge.

Its three components are: (a) every accessible state is structured signification; (b) participation occurs through a finite set of directional operations over that signification; (c) there is no extra-symbolic access channel available to the participant as participant.

Crucially, the claim is **falsifiable in principle**. It is refuted by exhibiting either an accessible participant state that is not structured signification, or a primitive directional operation that is neither a selection-change, nor a routing-change, nor a parameter of one. Its falsifiability is the form its accuracy-aspiration takes.

## Epistemic & alethic marks
- **E:** Derived, AC — the benchmark fixes this as the formal register.
- **A:** Aspires to map the actual structure of access, and is falsifiable in principle — refuted by exhibiting an accessible participant state that is not structured signification, or a primitive directional operation that is neither a selection-change, nor a routing-change, nor a parameter of one. Its falsifiability is the form its accuracy-aspiration takes.

## Distinctions & cautions
This claim must be kept distinct from the human gloss. Arguing from the rich gloss while defending with this thin claim is the prohibited error of **axiom equivocation**. The claim is the register you may defend with; the gloss carries no probative weight.
