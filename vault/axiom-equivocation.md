# Axiom Equivocation

> **The prohibited error of arguing from the rich human gloss while defending with the thin closure claim — and the controlling authority caps any text that fails to keep the two registers marked.**
> *Part I · AC (authority-canonical) · canonical*

## Definition
Axiom equivocation is the named, prohibited error of conflating the axiom's two registers — specifically, **arguing from the rich human gloss while defending with the thin closure claim.** Both registers must be preserved, and a reader must always mark which one is in use.

## In depth
The single axiom sentence splits into a **formal closure claim** (thin, load-bearing, the register you may defend with) and a **human gloss** (rich, phenomenological, exposition only, carrying no probative weight). Axiom equivocation is the maneuver of drawing argumentative force from the gloss's richness while retreating to the thin closure claim whenever challenged.

The error is not merely discouraged; it is **capped** by the controlling authority. The authority requires the split between registers and caps any text that fails to make it. This is a fact about the authority — contestable by contesting the authority, since canonical does not mean necessary — but as long as that authority controls, the cap stands.

The defense against the error is procedural and continuous: keep both registers preserved, and at every point mark which register is in use.

## Epistemic & alethic marks
- **E:** Derived, AC — the controlling authority requires the split and **caps** any text that fails to make it; contestable by contesting the authority. (Canonical ≠ necessary.)
- **A:** The split maps a real distinction between formal and phenomenological registers that any honest exposition must track.

## Distinctions & cautions
This is a **canonical** (authority-fixed) cap, not a metaphysical necessity — it is contestable by contesting the authority. It is NOT a claim that the gloss is worthless; the gloss is valid exposition. The error lies only in the equivocating *use*: force borrowed from the gloss, defense conducted from the closure claim.
